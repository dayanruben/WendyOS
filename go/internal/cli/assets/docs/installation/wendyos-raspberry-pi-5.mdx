WendyOS runs on the **Raspberry Pi 5**, **Raspberry Pi 4**, and **Raspberry Pi 3**. This guide walks you through installing it on any of the three. The Wendy agent comes prepackaged with WendyOS, so no separate agent installation is required.

## Supported Boards

| Board | Boot media | Connect to your computer via |
|-------|------------|------------------------------|
| Raspberry Pi 5 | microSD card or NVMe | USB-C cable or network |
| Raspberry Pi 4 | microSD card | USB-C cable or network |
| Raspberry Pi 3 (B / B+) | microSD card | Network only (Wi-Fi or Ethernet) |

<Callout type="info">
  The Pi 5 and Pi 4 expose a USB gadget over their USB-C port, so you can plug them straight into your computer for setup. The Pi 3 B/B+ cannot do this — its onboard USB hub blocks USB gadget mode — so it connects over the network instead. Plan to give the Pi 3 Wi-Fi or wired Ethernet (see the connection step below).
</Callout>

## Installation Steps

Prefer to follow along with video? Watch the [WendyOS Raspberry Pi 5 installation video](https://youtu.be/87GlYkx86jY). The steps are the same for the Pi 4 and Pi 3, apart from how you connect the board.

    </video>

    Then insert the adapter into your MacBook's SD card slot.

    <video width="100%" autoPlay loop muted playsInline>
      <source src="/videos/installation/pi-5-insert-card-into-macbook.mp4" type="video/mp4" />
      Your browser does not support the video tag.
    </video>

    <Callout type="info">
      **Pi 5 with NVMe**: If you're booting your Pi 5 from an NVMe drive instead of a microSD card, connect the NVMe drive to your computer (for example via a USB-C NVMe enclosure) and select it as the target drive in the next step.
    </Callout>
  </Step>

  <Step>
    ### Install WendyOS

    Use the `wendy` CLI to install the operating system:

    ```bash
    wendy os install
    ```

    Select your board — **Raspberry Pi 5**, **Raspberry Pi 4**, or **Raspberry Pi 3** — and then select your drive. You'll begin downloading a copy of WendyOS and writing it to your microSD card (or NVMe drive on the Pi 5). This can take a while. You'll need to enter your administrator password to write to the drive.

    <Callout type="info">
      **Pi 3 — set up Wi-Fi now**: The Pi 3 connects over the network rather than over USB, so it's easiest to pre-configure Wi-Fi while imaging. The installer will offer to do this, or you can pass it directly:

      ```bash
      wendy os install --wifi-ssid "MyNetwork" --wifi-password "hunter2"
      ```

      If you're using wired Ethernet on the Pi 3, you can skip this. Pi 5 and Pi 4 users can also pre-seed Wi-Fi the same way, but it's optional since those boards also connect over USB-C.
    </Callout>
  </Step>

  <Step>
    ### Insert the microSD Card into Your Pi

    Remove the card from your computer and insert the microSD card into your Raspberry Pi. (On a Pi 5 booting from NVMe, install the NVMe drive into your Pi 5's M.2 HAT instead.)

    <video width="100%" autoPlay loop muted playsInline>
      <source src="/videos/installation/pi-5-insert-micro-sd-card-to-pi.mp4" type="video/mp4" />
      Your browser does not support the video tag.
    </video>
  </Step>

  <Step>
    ### Connect and Power On Your Pi

    How you connect depends on your board:

    <Tabs items={['Raspberry Pi 5', 'Raspberry Pi 4', 'Raspberry Pi 3']} groupId="rpi-model" persist>
      <Tab value="Raspberry Pi 5">
        Connect your Raspberry Pi 5 to your computer using a USB-C cable. This both powers the board and creates a direct connection for deploying and managing apps.

        <video width="100%" autoPlay loop muted playsInline>
          <source src="/videos/installation/pi-5-usb-c-to-macbook.mp4" type="video/mp4" />
          Your browser does not support the video tag.
        </video>
      </Tab>

      <Tab value="Raspberry Pi 4">
        Connect your Raspberry Pi 4 to your computer using a USB-C cable, plugged into the Pi's USB-C port. The Pi 4's USB-C port runs in gadget mode, so this both powers the board and creates a direct connection for deploying and managing apps — just like the Pi 5.
      </Tab>

      <Tab value="Raspberry Pi 3">
        The Raspberry Pi 3 doesn't support a USB connection to your computer, so it connects over the network instead:

        1. Power the Pi 3 with its micro-USB power supply.
        2. Make sure it can reach your network — either over the Wi-Fi you configured during install, or by plugging in an Ethernet cable.

        Your computer and the Pi 3 must be on the same network for the next step to find it.
      </Tab>
    </Tabs>
  </Step>

  <Step>
    ### Discover Your Device

    Wait 1–2 minutes after powering on your Pi to allow WendyOS to fully boot. Then run:

    ```bash
    wendy discover
    # Set this device as your default
    wendy device set-default
    ```

    You should see your Raspberry Pi appear in the list of discovered devices.

    ![Wendy Discover](/images/docs/wendy-discover.png)

    <Callout type="warning">
      **Don't see your Pi 3?** Since the Pi 3 is found over the network, discovery relies on mDNS. If it doesn't show up, confirm the Pi is on the same network as your computer and that the network allows mDNS traffic — see [Discovering Devices](/docs/guides/device/discovering-devices) for troubleshooting.
    </Callout>

    Your device is now ready for development! For more information on device discovery and configuration, see [Discovering Devices](/docs/guides/device/discovering-devices).
  </Step>
</Steps>
